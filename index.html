About

I am a water-resources researcher interestied in how rivers work, connecting hydrology, fluvial geomorpology, and aquatic ecology. Most of my work integrates field data collection, geographic information systems (GIS), remote sensing (e.g., Landsat, MODIS, Sentinel products), and statistical and machine learning models to answer critical applied research questions. Deliverables from my work include interactive web applications, novel GIS tools, reproducible analysis pipelines, and analyses supporting peer reviewed research, all with the goal of helping water resource managers make science-based decisions about rivers in the Mountain West. What drives me is pretty simple: rivers are among the most biodiverse and most degraded ecosystems on the planet, and there's real work to do conserving and restoring them. I want to be part of that work in a way that's both scientifically rigorous and practically useful.

I like problems where the hard part isn't the model, it's the data: rare events, seasonal signals, leaky validation splits, and datasets that live behind cloud APIs. Most of my day-to-day is Python, geospatial tooling, and a healthy skepticism of my own results.

Projects

Great Salt Lake Functional Flows Explorer

ArcGIS Experience Builder · Utah State University

An interactive web app for exploring functional-flow metrics — peak flows, recessions, summer and winter low flows — across ~2,500 stream segments in the Great Salt Lake basin. Users pick a stream on a 3D map and see percentile tables of flow metrics by water-year type, plus hydrograph schematics comparing predicted and observed flow behavior at gaged sites.

SCAR: Satellite-based Classification of post-fire Alluvial Response

For decades researchers have lamented the fact that risk models for post fire debris flows are starved of the one thing they need most: an emperical record of when and where debris flows actually happened, and the associated storms that trigger them. Tis ongoing project aims to build that record from remotely-sensed data end to end. . The interesting challenges are rare-event class imbalance, time-aware cross-validation, and pushing feature engineering server-side in Earth Engine so iteration stays fast offline. 1. Find the debris flows. Date the debris flows. At each detected site, a hist-gradient-boosted decision tree model (with GEE server side feature engineering) reads multivariate time series of spectral indices and synthetic aperature radar, and pins down when the debris flow occurred. 3. Explain them. Wit when and where in hand, each event is attached to its drivers (precipitation, burn severity, terrain, geology) building the empirical training data te field currently lacks. a gradient-boosted 4. Learn from the data. A risk model trains on those features to predict what produces debris flows, and under what conditions. 5. Deploy at burn time. When a new fire burns, the model runs on the fresh burn scar, locally calibrated on older fires nearby producing hazard estimates grounded in what similar terrain actually did.
